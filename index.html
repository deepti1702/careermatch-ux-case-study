01 · Problem Statement

What Problem Were We Solving?

Early-career job seekers struggle to tailor resumes for each application. Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) filter out unoptimized resumes before they ever reach a human recruiter, creating an invisible barrier that disproportionately affects new graduates.

🤖
ATS Black Box
75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a human sees them. Candidates get no feedback on why or what to change.
🔄
High-Effort Customization
Manually tailoring each resume version for different roles is time-consuming, error-prone, and cognitively demanding for someone applying to dozens of positions.
😰
AI Without Guardrails
Existing AI resume tools make bulk edits without confirmation steps, leaving users anxious about what was changed and unable to undo mistakes.
📊
Opaque Scoring
Match scores are presented without context — users don't know if 72% is good or bad, or which specific gaps are costing them the most.
🎓
Riya Patel · Primary Persona
24-year-old CS graduate student in Boston, actively applying to SWE intern roles. Managing 3 resume versions across 15+ applications, under intense time pressure.

04 · Usability Testing

07 · AI Tools Used & Reflections

AI tools were integrated throughout every phase of the project — not as a shortcut, but as a design research and production accelerator. Each tool had a distinct role and specific outputs that fed directly into project deliverables.

Reflection on AI Effectiveness

AI tools were most effective when given structured, specific prompts with clear constraints. The heuristic evaluation prompt that specified task flows, heuristics, severity levels, and persona context produced immediately usable output. Generic prompts like "evaluate this app" produced shallow results. The biggest time savings came from transcript analysis and data extraction — tasks that would have taken 3–4 hours manually were completed in under 20 minutes. The key limitation: AI cannot replace watching users struggle in real time. P4's status badge confusion and P5's download discoverability issue were only visible because a human was observing — no heuristic evaluation surfaced these specific behaviors. The ideal workflow is AI for structured analysis + human observation for behavioral insight.

08 · Key Takeaways

What Would I Do Differently?

Six design and research principles emerged from this project that I'll carry forward into future work.

01
Trust is built before irreversible actions
The single highest-value change was adding a confirmation step before AI bulk edits. Users felt in control instantly. For any AI-powered tool, explicit consent moments are not friction — they are the product.
02
Affordance cues matter more on mobile
Two participants couldn't tell if the status badge was tappable. On desktop, hover states solve this. On mobile, you need visual conventions (chevrons, pill styles) baked into the component from the start.
03
AI evaluations miss behavioral edge cases
Heuristic evaluation found 18 structural issues. But the download discoverability problem and status badge confusion — both found only in live testing — wouldn't have been fixed without real users. AI accelerates; observation reveals.
04
Jargon kills UX silently
"Propagate Changes" confused two participants who didn't know what would happen. Renaming it "Apply to Other Versions" eliminated the confusion instantly. Plain language is a design decision that needs the same rigor as visual layout.
05
Sticky CTAs aren't optional on long screens
The download button disappearing on scroll in Task 4 nearly caused P5 to abandon the task. On mobile, any primary action that can scroll out of view should be sticky — this is especially critical for conversion-moment screens.
06
Iterative testing reveals what evaluation misses
The 4 post-testing prototype changes were small but high-impact. The lesson: the first round of testing is not the final state. Planning for at least one iteration cycle after testing should be built into every project timeline from the start.
